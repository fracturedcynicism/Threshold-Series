The Horizontal Descent

Atmospheric horror novella by Fractured Cynicism

✦
Part I

The Arrival

The airport exists in a state of persistent half-light. Not night, not day. The kind of illumination that makes you uncertain whether you have been here for minutes or hours. Arjun moves through the corridors with his suitcase, and the suitcase feels heavier than it did when he packed it. As if the air pressure of Calcutta has increased its weight. As if the city has already begun to press down on everything that enters it.

The taxi driver speaks a language that sounds like Bengali but has become something else. Something older. The words arrive in Arjun's ears and then he hears them again, as if the city is repeating what has been said, mocking the conversation from the walls themselves.

"First time back in how long?" the driver asks, but Arjun has already answered this question. He is certain he has already answered it. Multiple times. In multiple conversations. With multiple drivers. The road unfolding outside the window is familiar in a way that makes his stomach tighten. The buildings lean the same way. The power lines droop the same way. The vendor pushing his cart along the sidewalk moves with the same gesture he has always moved with. Nothing has changed. Nothing ever changes. And that immobility is the first hint that something is profoundly wrong.

"Three years," he hears himself say.

"Three years is long time to stay away from home."

But this is not his home. Home is a room forty floors above Bangalore. Home is a desk facing a window that shows a city that never stops moving. Home is the place where he learned that staying in one location was equivalent to death.

The taxi turns onto a street that is not the street he expected. The bougainvillea blooming from the balconies is the wrong shade of pink. Too vivid. Too insistent. As if the flowers are trying to communicate something through their color. As if they are screaming, and he is merely perceiving their scream as visual data.

His leg bounces against the taxi seat. The motion started without his intention. He tries to stop it. The effort of stopping it creates a tension that radiates up his spine. He releases the effort. The bouncing continues. His body is doing things that he does not authorize, and he is watching his body perform these unauthorized movements with the detachment of someone observing a stranger.

The taxi stops. The driver turns around. His face is in shadow. Why is his face in shadow? The afternoon light is streaming through the windows. But the driver's face remains obscured. Arjun has the sudden, ridiculous certainty that the driver has no face. That where a face should be, there is only the shadow that has learned to speak like a person.

"Here," the shadow says.

The apartment building is the correct building. The color is the correct color. The flowers are the right flowers, even though he knows they are the wrong flowers. His mother is visible through the open door. She is smaller than the memory of her, or perhaps the memory was larger than the reality, or perhaps time has rearranged the proportions of people the way it rearranges the proportions of cities.

He pays the driver too much, and the driver thanks him with an intonation that suggests gratitude mixed with something else. Something darker. Something that sounds like pity.

The apartment smells exactly as it smelled when he was eighteen years old. The smell has not changed. How is this possible? Smells dissipate. Smells transform. But this smell—a combination of dal cooking, incense burned years ago and soaked into the fabric of the walls, the particular dust of a room that has been inhabited by the same people for too long—this smell is absolutely preserved.

His mother hugs him. She is making sounds that might be words. His father appears in the doorway, and they perform the ritual of greeting that their family has always performed. His father places a hand on his shoulder. The hand is smaller than it should be. Or his shoulder is larger. The proportions of his body have become unreliable.

The room is waiting. His room. The bed where he slept as a teenager. The window that looks out onto Lower Circular Road. The water stain on the ceiling in the shape of something he has never been able to identify. A face, perhaps. A thing that is not a face but resembles one closely enough to be unsettling.

He sets his suitcase on the floor. The suitcase makes a sound. A soft sound. But the sound seems to linger in the room longer than sound should linger. The sound is still echoing when he hears his mother asking him about the flight, about Bangalore, about the work that keeps him vertical and moving and alive in a way that has nothing to do with being alive.

His leg is bouncing again.

His fingers are moving, tapping against his thigh in a rhythm that he cannot quite identify. Is it a pattern? Is it random? Or is it a message being sent by his body to someone, or something, that he cannot perceive?

✦

Night comes, or evening transitions into the time that is called night in this city, though the darkness is not complete. The darkness is interrupted by the sodium-vapor lights on the street. The darkness is interrupted by the light spilling from other apartments. The darkness is interrupted by the ever-present hum of the city, which is not quite sound but more like the vibration of something breathing.

Dinner is a ritual. The table is too small. The space between the place settings is too large. There should be four place settings. His mother has set only three. Ananya's absence is more present than any physical object could be.

His father talks about the news. About work. About the monsoons. His father is performing the function of a father—filling the space with words, creating a structure that might contain the silence. But the words are arriving at Arjun's ears in a language that is increasingly unfamiliar. Not unfamiliar in the sense that he does not understand the words. Unfamiliar in the sense that the words are carrying weight they should not carry. Carrying meaning beneath their meaning.

His mother watches him eat. She is analyzing his consumption of food the way anthropologists analyze the behavior of foreign creatures. She is trying to determine something through the observation of his jaw movements, the rhythm of his swallowing, the way his hand moves from plate to mouth.

"Ananya was asking about you," his mother says, and the words arrive like a small hammer striking glass.

"Was she."

"She wanted me to tell you she hopes you're doing well."

This is a lie. This is what a lie looks like when it is told by someone who loves you and is trying to protect you from a truth that is more cutting than any honesty could be. Ananya is not thinking of him. Ananya has moved on. Ananya is in Delhi now, and she is happy, and that happiness is not his responsibility, and the relief he should feel about this is replaced by something that feels like the floor opening beneath him.

He sets his fork down carefully. The fork makes a sound against the plate. The sound is too loud. Everything is too loud. The hum of the refrigerator is a roar. The tick of the clock is a metronome counting the moments until something catastrophic occurs.

"I need to rest," he says.

He excuses himself before anyone can respond. He climbs the stairs to the second floor, and the stairs are steeper than they were. Or they are the correct steepness, and his lungs are less capable of handling the climb. By the time he reaches the bedroom, he is breathing hard, as if he has run a great distance, rather than climbed a single flight of stairs.

The door does not close all the way. He tries to shut it. It stops halfway. Something is preventing it from closing. He does not investigate. Investigation would require him to accept that something external to him is interfering with the basic functions of the building. It is easier to simply accept the half-closed door. Easier to lie on the bed and stare at the ceiling where the water stain is moving, or appears to be moving, or has been moving so slowly that only now does he notice the movement.

The water stain is definitely different from how he remembers it. It has developed features. Eyes, perhaps. A mouth. The stain is becoming recognizable as something that resembles a face, though not a human face. Something older. Something that has been looking down at him while he slept all these years, and now it is finally being seen.

His phone buzzes. A message from work. Numbers and projections. The world continuing in Bangalore without him. The world that makes sense. The world where cause and effect are reliable. The world that is receding from him with each breath he takes.

He does not respond.

He lies in the darkness, and the darkness is not complete. The darkness is filled with the residue of light. The darkness is a presence. And slowly, as he lies there, he begins to perceive that the darkness is not empty. The darkness is inhabited.

Part II

The Unraveling

Days collapse into themselves. Or perhaps only minutes have passed. The calendar suggests that it is the fourth day since his arrival. The fourth day, and yet he has memories of breakfast with his mother that feel like they happened weeks ago. He has memories of conversations that he is uncertain occurred. He has memories of walking through Calcutta, but the Calcutta he walked through had streets that led to themselves. Streets that repeated. Streets that seemed to acknowledge his presence with a kind of hostile recognition.

The apartment has a rhythm. His mother comes to the bedroom at regular intervals. She brings food. She asks him if he slept. She suggests that he come downstairs. He refuses politely. She leaves. Time passes. She returns.

Or perhaps she never leaves. Perhaps the intervals are simply his consciousness becoming aware and unaware in cycles. Perhaps his mother is a constant presence, and he is simply flickering in and out of the ability to perceive her.

His leg shakes. His fingers tap. His breath comes in increments that do not seem to match the oxygen requirements of his body. There is too much breath. There is not enough breath. He is breathing air that is too thin, despite the fact that the air in the apartment is the same air that it has always been.

He tries to read. He picks up a book from the nightstand. The words on the page are arranged correctly. They form sentences. But the sentences are not making sense. Or they are making too much sense. They are making a sense that is deeper than the surface meaning of the words. They are saying something about him. About the nature of his return. About the impossibility of escape.

He sets the book down and stares at the ceiling. The water stain is definitely different. It has developed dimension. It is no longer a flat mark on the plaster. It has become a thing that exists in three dimensions. It is protruding from the ceiling. Or his perception of three-dimensional space has become unreliable, and what he is seeing is a two-dimensional stain rendered as three-dimensional through some failure of his eyes or his mind.

The phone rings. The sound is sharp. It cuts through the hum of the apartment like a blade. He reaches for the phone, but his hand does not obey immediately. When it finally completes the movement, the phone has stopped ringing. He holds the receiver to his ear anyway. There is silence on the line. But the silence is not empty. The silence is full of presence.

"Arjun?" His father's voice, but distant, as if coming from another room, or from another city, or from the past.

"Yes."

"Are you all right?"

Is he all right? He considers the question. He considers what "all right" might mean in this context. In this building. In this city. In this body that is performing movements without his authorization.

"I'm fine," he says. "Just tired."

His father is quiet for a moment. The quiet is the quiet of someone deciding how much truth they can speak. How much they can reveal without causing further damage.

"Your mother is worried about you."

"I know."

"She says you're not eating."

"I'm not hungry."

"You need to eat. You need to take care of yourself."

But care-taking is a concept that seems to belong to a different version of reality. In this reality, in this apartment, care-taking is impossible. Care-taking would require him to still exist in a way that is concrete enough to benefit from nourishment. And he is becoming less and less certain about the concreteness of his existence.

After the conversation ends, or perhaps while it is still happening—time is becoming unreliable—he lies back on the bed. The mattress has a smell. It is the smell of years of sleeping. The smell of his body, reproduced infinitely, soaked into the fabric. He is lying in the accumulated smell of every version of himself that has ever slept in this location.

Through the window, the city is doing something. The sounds of Calcutta are arriving in waves. The honking of cars. The voices of people in the street. The sound of a bicycle bell. The sound of a child crying. The sound of someone speaking in a language that might be Bengali, or might be something that only sounds like Bengali. All of these sounds are happening simultaneously. They are not layered. They are not occurring in sequence. They are all present at the same instant, and his brain is struggling to parse them into something that resembles a coherent soundscape.

He closes his eyes. Behind his eyelids, he sees the water stain. It has color now. It is brown and gray and something that might be red, though the red could be a visual artifact produced by the strain of looking at something that is not meant to be seen directly.

His hands are shaking so badly that he has to grip the mattress to keep them still. And even gripping the mattress, the shaking continues. It is not his hands that are shaking. It is the mattress that is shaking. It is the bed that is trembling beneath him. Or it is the building. Or it is the city itself that has developed some kind of fever, and the shaking is the expression of that fever, and he is simply experiencing it in the same way that a microorganism experiences the fever of the body it inhabits.

He does not remember leaving the apartment. But he is on a street. A street in Calcutta that is somehow both familiar and impossible. The buildings are in the wrong locations. Or they are in the correct locations, but they have rearranged themselves when he was not looking.

There is a man walking ahead of him. The man is wearing a blue shirt. The man has the gait of someone who belongs to this city. The man does not look back, but Arjun has the absolutely certainty that the man is aware of his presence. That the man is leading him somewhere. That the man has been leading him for longer than Arjun can remember.

He tries to call out to the man. His voice does not work. Or his voice works, but the words come out as something other than what he intended to speak. They come out as a language that he does not recognize but that sounds like it should be Bengali, the way a photograph of someone can resemble them without being them.

The street is changing. The light is changing. The quality of the air is changing. The city is becoming something that it was and something that it is becoming simultaneously. And Arjun is moving through this transformation, and he cannot determine whether he is causing the transformation or whether the transformation is causing him.

The man in the blue shirt stops walking. He stands at the intersection of two streets. Arjun comes to a stop several paces behind him. The man does not turn around. But he speaks.

"You remember me," the man says.

Arjun does not respond. He cannot respond, because responding would mean accepting a level of unreality that he is not prepared to accept.

"I am you," the man continues. "From before you left."

The words are forming a shape that has meaning, but the meaning is not arriving through language. The meaning is arriving through something deeper. Through something that is below the level of rational thought.

"I don't understand," Arjun says, and his voice sounds like it is coming from very far away.

"You will," the man says. "Everyone understands eventually. The city makes sure of it."

When Arjun looks up, the man is gone. There is only the street. And the street is beginning to fold in on itself. The perspective is becoming unreliable. The buildings are taller on one side of the street and shorter on the other side, though they are the same buildings. The light is arriving at a different angle on different sides of the same intersection, though there is only one sun.

He does not remember walking back to the apartment. He does not remember climbing the stairs. But he is in the bedroom, and it is night, and the water stain on the ceiling is moving, is definitely, absolutely moving, the movement is not a hallucination, the movement is the one thing in the apartment that is most real, the water stain is moving like something alive, like something that has been trapped in the ceiling for years and is finally, slowly, extricating itself.

His phone shows that it is 3:47 AM. Or it is 3:47 PM. The display is difficult to read. The numbers are rearranging themselves.

He does not sleep.

Part III

The Dissolution

Time has become a concept that is difficult to maintain. He knows that days have passed. His mother has mentioned this. She has said things like "You've been here a week" and "It's been ten days." But the internal experience of time is not matching the external markers. He has lived through weeks in what he remembers as hours. He has lived through minutes that feel like years.

His body is changing. He notices this in the mirror, though the mirror is not entirely reliable. The mirror shows him as he looks, but it also shows him as he looked, at various ages, all simultaneously. He sees himself at ten years old, at twenty, at thirty, all of them overlapping, all of them looking back at him with expressions that suggest they know something he does not.

He is eating less. Or he is not eating at all. The food arrives, and he moves it around on the plate, and then his mother takes the plate away. He is not certain whether he has consumed any food in the past several days. His stomach does not feel empty. It feels compressed. It feels like there is a weight sitting on it that is preventing it from functioning correctly.

His mother's face is becoming more lined. Or perhaps she was always this lined, and he is only now capable of perceiving the lines. She looks at him with an expression that combines worry and something else. Something that might be fear. The fear of recognizing something in the face of her son that is not quite compatible with him being her son.

His father has stopped coming to the bedroom. His father lingers in the doorway sometimes, but he does not enter. It is as if he has decided that the room has become contaminated, and to enter would be to expose himself to whatever it is that is happening to Arjun.

The leg bouncing has stopped. His fingers have stopped tapping. He has not noticed when this happened. But at some point, the constant, unauthorized movements ceased. Now he is still. The stillness is somehow more terrifying than the movement was. The stillness suggests that some process has been completed. That some transition has occurred.

He walks to the mirror. He looks at himself. The face that looks back is approximately his face. The features are arranged correctly. But something about the arrangement is wrong. The eyes are too far apart, or too close together, or perhaps they are exactly the correct distance apart and the error is in his perception of distance. The mouth is familiar but also alien, as if it has been rearranged slightly, so subtly that only he would notice, but notice he has.

He touches the glass. The glass is cold. His fingers are not cold. The temperature difference is absolute and impossible. His hand should warm the glass, should reach equilibrium. But the glass remains cold, and his fingers remain something other than cold, and the two are not bridging the gap between them.

When he pulls his hand away, there is a mark on the mirror. Not a fingerprint. Something else. A trace of something that his hand has left behind. The mark remains even after he wipes it. It is becoming darker. It is becoming more defined. It is becoming the outline of a hand that is not quite his hand, but close enough.

✦

The apartment is changing. The walls have become textured in a way that they were not before. There are patterns in the texture. Or the texture is creating patterns in his vision. When he looks directly at the wall, it is simply a wall. When he looks at it peripherally, it is something else. It is moving. It is breathing. It is responding to his presence in subtle ways.

The bathroom sink has begun to produce water that is not quite water. It is viscous. It is the color of something that he cannot name. It smells of rust and something older. Something that smells like time accumulating. He washes his hands in the water anyway. There is no alternative. All the water in the city has become this water, or all the water in the apartment, or perhaps only this water exists, and he is imagining the water from before.

The toilet makes sounds. Strange, gurgling sounds. The sounds are rhythmic. The sounds are almost like breathing. He does not use the toilet anymore. He uses the sink in the corner of the bedroom. Or he does not urinate. Or he does urinate, and his body is handling the elimination in a way that no longer involves the normal processes. Everything is becoming automatic. Everything is becoming less visible. He is becoming less visible.

His mother has stopped asking him to come downstairs. She has stopped insisting that he eat. She has stopped trying to engage him in conversation. Now she comes to the room and stands in the doorway and looks at him with an expression that suggests she is trying to remember something. Trying to remember what her son looked like before he became this thing that is living in her apartment.

She has called someone. He is aware of this. He has heard her voice on the phone, speaking words like "hospitalization" and "psychiatric" and "not responding." She has called someone, and that someone will presumably arrive at some point. But time is no longer operating on a schedule that allows for predictable arrivals.

The window is becoming difficult to see through. It is not that the glass is becoming opaque. It is that the world beyond the window is becoming less visible. The city is beginning to retreat. Or he is beginning to retreat from the city. Or the boundary between himself and the city is beginning to dissolve, and as it dissolves, the distinction between inside and outside is becoming meaningless.

He tries to read. He tries to think. Both activities are becoming more difficult. The words on the page are not assembling themselves into meaning anymore. Or the meaning is too clear. The meaning is arriving too directly. The words are bypassing the language centers of his brain and going directly to some deeper place, a place that understands meaning without needing to translate it through words.

His thoughts are becoming less sequential. They are beginning to overlap. He is thinking about Ananya and Bangalore and his mother and the man in the blue shirt, all at the same time, all with equal weight and importance. The thoughts are not creating a narrative. They are simply co-existing in the space of his mind, like multiple radio stations broadcasting simultaneously.

Part IV

The Enclosure

It is difficult to know when the transition occurred. At some point, he stopped leaving the room. At some point, the room stopped being a place that he could leave. The boundaries between leaving and staying became theoretical rather than practical.

The door is still there. It is still open, slightly, just as it has been since he arrived. But there is something on the other side of the door that he cannot quite identify. It is not the hallway. Or it is the hallway, but the hallway has become something else. Something that is no longer oriented in a direction that he can move through.

His mother still brings food. The food appears on the nightstand. He does not eat it. The food sits there for a period of time, and then it disappears. His mother comes back and removes the untouched plate. She says things. Words arrive in his ears. He understands them on some level, but the understanding is not creating response. The response mechanisms are shutting down.

The water stain on the ceiling has become the most real thing in the apartment. It is no longer a stain. It is a presence. It is something that is looking down at him from the ceiling, something that has been looking down at him for years, through all the years of his life, and now it is finally manifesting in a form that he can perceive directly.

He stares at it. The stain stares back. This is not metaphorical. This is not poetic language masking absence. The stain has developed eyes, and the eyes have developed focus, and the focus is directed at him, and he understands that this gaze has been constant, unchanging, for all the time he has been alive.

The realization is not new. The realization is very old. He has always known that he is being watched. By the city. By the building. By something that exists in the spaces between moments.

His skin feels like paper. He can see the veins beneath the skin. They are blue and purple and other colors that are not standard vein colors. They are moving slowly. The movement is like water flowing through channels. He is beginning to suspect that he is no longer entirely contained within his skin. Something of him is leaking out. Some portion of his consciousness is beginning to exist outside of the boundaries of his body.

The apartment is becoming smaller. Or he is becoming larger. Or the distinction between size and space has become meaningless. The walls are closer. The ceiling is lower. The floor is more present beneath him. He is being compressed. He is being reduced. He is being condensed into something that is more essential.

He no longer tries to understand what is happening. Understanding would require a level of consciousness that is no longer available to him. He is existing in a state that is beyond understanding. He is existing in the way that objects exist. In the way that walls exist. In the way that water stains exist. Present. Eternal. Watching.

His phone has not buzzed in days. Or it has buzzed, and the sound has not reached him. Or the phone no longer exists in a form that can produce sound. The world of Bangalore is no longer broadcasting. The world of work is no longer reaching him. The vertical orientation of his life has become impossible to maintain. He is becoming horizontal. He is becoming flat. He is becoming a shadow.

Part V

The Conversation

There is a man sitting in the corner of the room. Arjun is not certain when the man arrived. The man has always been there. The man is the man in the blue shirt. The man is him, or was him, or could have been him in a city where different choices were possible.

"You're aware," the man says.

Arjun does not respond. But the response is implicit in the fact of his continued consciousness. Awareness is happening. It is continuing despite the dissolution of everything else.

"This is what everyone fears," the man continues. "Being trapped between things. Being unable to return to what was. Being unable to move forward to what might be. Just suspended here. In the gap."

"I'm not trapped," Arjun hears himself say. His voice is strange in his ears. It is thinner than it should be. It is a voice that has lost resonance.

"You're very trapped," the man says. "You have been trapped since you arrived. Perhaps since you left. Perhaps since you were born."

"Who are you?" The question arrives without intention.

"I'm the man who stayed in Calcutta. I'm the Arjun who chose differently. I'm the version of you that succeeded at horizontal existence. And I'm here to tell you that there is no difference between us. We both ended up here. We both became trapped by the very structure of trying to be a person in a place that does not want us."

The man is not entirely present. Or he is more present than Arjun is. His edges are not quite defined. He seems to exist slightly out of phase with the room. Like he is occupying the same space but in a slightly different configuration of reality.

"How long have I been here?" Arjun asks.

The man considers this. "Time is not meaningful in this context. You could have been here for hours. You could have been here for years. You could have always been here. The calendar on the wall is not reliable. The calendar in your mind is not reliable. All that is reliable is the fact of your presence in this room."

"Is my mother real?"

"Is anyone real?" the man responds. "The question becomes more complicated the longer you ask it. Your mother is real insofar as she is having an experience of believing in your reality. But her experience is degrading. Soon, she will no longer be able to convince herself that her son is still present. Then you will become a memory. And memories are a different category of reality. Less defined. More flexible. More true."

"When will this end?"

"This has already ended," the man says. "This is the aftermath. This is the part that comes after the ending, when the echoes of the ending are still reverberating. This is not life. But it is not death either. It is something between. Something that the language has not developed a word for."

✦

His mother comes to the door. She looks at him, and her face is showing something that he does not have words for. Concern that has become resignation. Love that has curdled into something that resembles duty without the hope that duty ordinarily carries.

"The doctor is coming tomorrow," she says. "Or today. Or he came already. We're not quite certain anymore."

"I'm fine," Arjun says, though he is aware that he is no longer fine, that fine has become a concept that no longer applies to him, that he has transcended the possibility of being fine.

"I know," his mother says. And the terrible thing is that she does know. She knows that he is not fine, and she knows that fine is no longer a relevant category, and she knows that what is happening to him is beyond the reach of anything that medicine can accomplish.

She closes the door. Or the door closes itself. Or the door has always been closed, and he has only been imagining the slight opening that allowed him to perceive the hallway beyond.

The room is complete now. It is a closed system. He is inside it. There is no outside anymore. Or the outside has become irrelevant. Or he has become too essential, too reduced, to reach the outside anymore.

He lies on the bed, and the bed holds him, and the holding is the only thing that is keeping him from dispersing entirely into the air of the apartment.

Part VI

The Cessation

He is not certain whether he is breathing anymore. The breath is still happening, or the memory of breath is continuing, or some process similar to breathing is maintaining the basic functions that keep consciousness active.

The water stain has spread. It is no longer confined to a single spot on the ceiling. It is moving across the plaster like a living thing. Like something that is hunting. The edges are becoming more defined. The center is becoming darker. It is taking on the appearance of something that is almost recognizable. An animal. A creature. A thing that should not exist in the world of physics and biology as he understands them, but clearly does.

The light in the room is changing. It is becoming the light of a time of day that does not correspond to any actual time. It is the light of afternoon, but also of evening, but also of the time just before dawn. It is all of these times simultaneously, which is no time at all, which is time becoming unstable, which is time beginning to recognize its own fundamental unreality.

His father came to the door at some point. His father stood in the doorway and looked at him, and there was nothing left in his father's expression except the recognition that something he had created, something he had invested decades in maintaining, had become something else entirely. His father turned around and left, and Arjun understood that this was the last time he would see his father, not because his father was dying, but because Arjun had become invisible, had become something that could no longer be perceived by the mechanisms that human eyes possessed.

The work in Bangalore has moved on. He knows this without knowing how he knows. The Morrison project is complete. The promotion that was supposed to be his has been given to someone else. The world of vertical aspiration continues without him. The world of achievement continues. The world of meaning continues. But he is no longer a part of it. He has fallen out of the world. Or the world has rearranged itself to exclude him. Or he has always been excluded, and he is only now noticing the exclusion.

Ananya has had a child. He knows this without seeing it, without hearing it from his mother or anyone else. The knowledge arrives like a memory of something that happened in the past, though he is aware that the past is no longer a place that he can reliably reference.

The child is named Arjun.

The knowledge of this carries a weight that disproportionate to the factual content. The name will persist. The name will continue. But Arjun will not. Arjun will remain here, in this room, in this building, in this city that does not want him and never wanted him, that he tried to escape and that he has returned to and that is now holding him in a grip that cannot be released because the grip has become the only thing that is keeping him from falling entirely into absence.

He is very still now. Not moving. Not performing actions. Simply existing in a state of ongoing awareness. The awareness is not comfortable. The awareness is not accompanied by the possibility of action or change or escape. The awareness is simply happening, continuing, persisting in the face of the fact that there is nothing to be aware of except the awareness itself.

Hours or days or years pass. They pass in the same way that water moves through a tube. The flow is constant. The flow is inevitable. The flow does not care whether the tube is conscious of the movement.

He tries to remember what Bangalore looked like. The memory is intact, but it is becoming more like a dream. The colors are shifting. The proportions are becoming unrealistic. The buildings are becoming taller than they were. The light is becoming stranger. The Bangalore he is remembering is not the Bangalore that exists. It is becoming a different place entirely. It is becoming Calcutta. All places are becoming Calcutta. All places are becoming this room.

His mother still comes to the door sometimes. She brings food that he does not eat. She speaks words that he does not hear. She is performing the ritual of having a son, even though the performance is no longer producing results. Even though her son is no longer capable of response.

The ritual is for her, not for him. The ritual is about maintaining the fiction that there is a son to maintain. The ritual is about refusing to acknowledge that the person in the bed is no longer quite a person, is no longer quite living, is no longer quite connected to the world in any way that would allow for meaningful interaction.

But she continues. She continues because continuing is what mothers do. Because stopping would mean accepting that something irreversible has occurred.

Part VII

The Boundary

The water stain has become so dark that it is almost invisible. The darkness is absolute. It is not dark in the way that shadows are dark. It is dark in the way that absence is dark. It is the visual representation of something that has been removed.

When he looks at it, he feels a pulling sensation. Not painful. Not unpleasant. Simply a pulling sensation, as if the stain is exerting a gentle gravity, is calling him toward something, is offering him a doorway into another state of being.

The man in the blue shirt is still sitting in the corner. Or the man in the blue shirt has never left. Or there has never been a man in the blue shirt, and the corner has always been empty, and the emptiness has always been speaking in a voice that Arjun has mistaken for another person.

"It doesn't hurt," the not-man says. Or speaks. Or communicates through means that are beyond the category of speech.

"I know," Arjun hears himself respond.

"Most people are afraid of the dissolution. They resist. They create pain through the resistance. But you have stopped resisting. You have achieved a state that most people will never achieve. The state of acceptance without hope."

"Is this death?" Arjun asks, though he is aware that the question is not quite appropriate, that death is a word that refers to a transition that he has already passed beyond.

"Death is a concept for people who are alive. You are beyond that concept now. You are in the space after concepts. You are in the place where language breaks down and words become irrelevant."

Arjun considers this. Or the considering is happening, and his consciousness is observing the considering from a distance, as if he is watching someone else think his thoughts.

"Will it always be like this?" he asks.

The not-man does not respond. The silence extends. The silence fills the room. The silence becomes the room. And Arjun understands that this is the answer. That yes, it will always be like this. That he has entered a state that has no expiration date. That he will continue to exist in this room, in this state of aware dissolution, for as long as consciousness continues to function, for as long as something that might be called Arjun continues to exist in any meaningful sense.

He lies on the bed, and he understands that he will die here. That his body will remain here. That his mother will eventually stop bringing food. That his mother will eventually stop coming to the door. That his mother will eventually die, and there will be no one left to maintain the fiction that there is a person in the room.

And after that, he will continue. The consciousness will continue. The awareness will persist. And he will remain in this room, watching the water stain, waiting for something that will never arrive, existing in a state of perpetual return, trapped in the moment of vacation that never ends, never resolves, never releases him into whatever state comes after dissolution.

The window is closed now. Or it has always been closed, and he has only been imagining the possibility of an outside. The sounds of Calcutta are no longer audible. The city has moved on. Or the city is still there, but he has moved into a place where the sounds cannot reach him.

Time has stopped meaning anything. The clock on the wall is still ticking, but the ticks are no longer corresponding to minutes or hours. They are corresponding to something else. To the rhythm of his own deterioration. To the pulse of his own dissolution.

His body is no longer entirely present. Or it is entirely present, but it is no longer entirely material. It is becoming translucent. It is becoming a suggestion of a body rather than a body itself. It is becoming a memory of what a body is.

And his mother continues to come to the door. She continues to bring food. She continues to speak words that might be, "Come down for dinner," or might be, "I miss you," or might be, "What happened to my son?"

He cannot respond. He has moved beyond the capacity for response. He has entered into the state of perfect passivity. The state of eternal waiting. The state of knowing that help will never arrive, that escape is impossible, that this room will be his world for as long as worlds continue to exist.

The water stain is expanding. It is covering the entire ceiling. It is beginning to move down the walls. It is beginning to envelop the room. The room is becoming dark. The room is becoming something else. The room is becoming him.

And he understands, in the final moment of understanding that will ever be available to him, that the room has always been him. That he has not been living in the room. That the room has been living in him. That the boundary between the container and the contained has dissolved long ago.

He is the apartment. He is the city. He is the accumulation of every moment that he has ever lived in this place. He is the returning. He is the inability to escape. He is the perfect and perpetual trap.

And it is not the city that has imprisoned him. It is not Calcutta that has held him. It is himself. It is the fundamental architecture of his own consciousness. The chain that binds him is made of his own choices. The trap is the inevitable consequence of attempting to escape the human condition through vertical aspiration and work and the delusion that meaning can be manufactured through the accumulation of achievement.

Final State

His mother stands at the doorway. She is no longer young. She is becoming old. She is becoming very old. He is aware that years have passed. Decades, perhaps. The passage of time is irrelevant, but the visual evidence of its passing is undeniable.

She looks at the bed. The bed is empty, or contains the suggestion of a shape beneath the blanket, or contains nothing at all. It is not entirely clear. The distinction between presence and absence has become too fine to measure.

"Arjun," she says. The word is a question. The word is a prayer. The word is a refusal to accept what has become obvious.

He is aware of her. He is always aware of her. Her presence is the only anchor that keeps him from dissolving entirely. But he can no longer respond. The mechanisms of response are no longer available to him. He has transcended the possibility of communicating back across the boundary that separates them.

She sets food on the nightstand. The food is no longer necessary. Neither of them believes that the food will be eaten. But the ritual continues. The ritual is the only thing left. The ritual is the only thing that maintains the fiction that there is still a son on the other side of her maternal devotion.

"I hope you're doing well," she says, though she knows he is not doing well, that the concept of "well" has become irrelevant, that what is happening on the other side of her understanding is something that language has no words for.

She leaves. The door closes. Or it closes the way it has always been closed. Slightly ajar. Sufficient enough to allow him to perceive the hallway, which is no longer there, which is still there, which never was there.

And he remains. He remains in the bed. He remains in the room. He remains in the city. He remains in the state of waiting for something that will never arrive.

The water stain on the ceiling is complete. It is no longer a stain. It is the ceiling itself. It is the defining feature of the room. It is the eye that is forever looking down. It is the presence that is forever watching.

And Arjun is beneath it, always beneath it, never moving toward escape or toward acceptance because the two have become indistinguishable. He is the man who went on vacation and could not return. He is the returning that never ends. He is the chain.

The chain is his. The chain is him. The chain is everything that remains when the possibility of being alive has been exhausted.

He does not sleep. He does not wake. He exists in the threshold. He exists in the moment between. He exists in the vacation that never ends, the room that never opens, the city that never releases him.

This is not a tragedy. This is not a cautionary tale. This is simply what happens to consciousness when it becomes trapped in the gap between the life it was supposed to live and the life it actually lived. This is the inevitable consequence of the choice to run from. This is the inescapable geometry of the room that was always there, waiting.

The chain continues.

The water stain grows darker.

The awareness persists.

And nothing changes.

Nothing ever changes.
